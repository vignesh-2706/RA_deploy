What is Rheumatoid Arthritis RA ?

Rheumatoid arthritis, or RA, is an autoimmune and inflammatory disease, which means that your immune system attacks healthy cells in your body by mistake, causing inflammation (painful swelling) in the affected parts of the body.

Symptoms

Pain or aching in more than one joint , Stiffness in more than one joint , Tenderness and swelling in more than one joint , The same symptoms on both sides of the body (such as in both hands or both knees) , Weight loss , Fever , Fatigue or tiredness , Weakness

Causes

RA is the result of an immune response in which the body’s immune system attacks its own healthy cells. The specific causes of RA are unknown, but some factors can increase the risk of developing the disease.

Complications

Premature heart disease - People with RA are also at a higher risk for developing other chronic diseases such as heart disease and diabetes.
Obesity - People with RA who are obese have an increased risk of developing heart disease risk factors such as high blood pressure and high cholesterol.

Diagnosis

Rheumatoid arthritis can be difficult to diagnose in its early stages because the early signs and symptoms mimic those of many other diseases. There is no one blood test or physical finding to confirm the diagnosis. The various diagnosis are listed below.

  • Physical exam
  • Antibody blood tests
  • Complete blood count
  • Imaging tests
  • C-reactive protein

Physical exam

The doctor will check your joints for swelling, tenderness, and range of motion. RA tends to strike several joints.

Antibody blood tests

Doctors look for certain proteins that show up in your blood when you have RA. These proteins mistakenly target healthy cells and kick off the inflammation process. So a high or positive test result means inflammation is in your body.

  • Rheumatoid factor (RF): high levels (over 20 u/ml)
  • Anti-CCP (anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide): high levels (over 20 u/ml)
  • ANA, or antinuclear antibodies: the results are positive or negative

Complete blood count

It helps your doctor find anemia (low red blood cells), which is common in RA. It looks for four things:

  1. White blood cells 4.8-10.8
  2. Red blood cells 4.7-6.1
  3. Hemoglobin 14.0-18.0
  4. Hematocrit 42-52
  5. Platelets 150-450

Imaging tests

These can help your doctor judge how severe your disease is and track its progress over time.

  1. X-ray can show whether (and how much) joint damage you have, though damage may not show up early on.
  2. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and ultrasound give a more detailed picture of your joints. These scans aren’t normally used to diagnose RA, but they can help doctors find it early.

C-reactive protein

This test measures levels of a protein your liver makes when inflammation is present. Results vary from person to person and lab to lab, but most of the time a normal result is less than 1.0.

Frequently Asked Questions

Below we have listed few frequently asked questions on Rheumatoid Arthritis.

  • How is rheumatoid arthritis (RA) different from some other forms of arthritis?

    Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) differs from some other forms of arthritis because it is symmetrical, affecting both sides of the body. Other types of arthritis, such as osteoarthritis, may affect only one side of the body.

  • Who is more affected by rheumatoid arthritis? Men or women?

    Rheumatoid arthritis tends to affect three times as many women as men, and symptoms may be more severe in women as well.RA tends to affect women at earlier ages, and men seem to have more cases of remission of the disease.

  • Surgery is the only way to treat RA. True or False?

    False. Treatment for rheumatoid arthritis usually involves a combination of medication, exercise, rest, and protecting the joints. Surgery may be needed in some cases.

  • Rheumatoid arthritis can be cured if diagnosed and treated early. True or False?

    False. Currently, there is no cure for rheumatoid arthritis. Treatment for RA that is started early on in the disease process can help minimize or slow damage to the joints and improve quality of life for patients.

  • What are early signs and symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis (RA)?

    In early stages of rheumatoid arthritis, the inflammation in the joints can cause tenderness and pain.There may not be as much redness or swelling as there is in later stages. Other early signs of rheumatoid arthritis may include joint stiffness that lasts at least 30 minutes, especially in the mornings. Some patients may have flare-ups, which can include fever, loss of appetite, and fatigue.
